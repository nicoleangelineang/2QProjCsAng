NFTs: ADVANTAGEOUS OR DETRIMENTAL?

The Ongoing debate between the Anti-NFTs and NFT Supporters


The balancing game between the pros and cons of NFTs is a very debated topic for its pros are undeniable. Afterall, it is a major market that has opened up opportunities for anyone of any social status to capitalize on, has provided a place for art collectors to extend their hobby digitally, and has, in no doubt, changed so many lives for the better - but whether these perks justify the impact it has on the environment through its extravagant energy consumption is very debatable. And whether they should be allowed to do what they have been essentially doing - destroying the world teraWatt hour by teraWatt hour - is a question everyone has yet to find a general answer to.

But there need not be a one-all be-all response to whether it's okay, because there are many views to the controversy that are heavily influenced by whether you are a seller, a collector, or someone merely looking in from outside the NFT marketplace. However, it still is important to form a personal opinion to something as serious of an issue as this - to look at both sides and judge objectively the major points that people side with to defend their stances. Here are the major ones for those in favor, and those not.



ADVANTAGES

The Pro-NFT Argument

  • For artists and creatives, it is a very lucrative market that helps them access a potential audience for their work. It is a very good source of income and revenue.
  • For artists and creatives, it gives them full copyright and protection from having their work stolen, as the metadata cannot be altered.
  • For collectors, it gives them the pride of ownership, of having something limited and unlike any other image on the internet.

LUCRATIVE AND DECENTRALIZED MARKET

The NFT market is a platform where miracles can happen. The earliest transaction that initially blew up the NFT scenes was when Mark Winkellman, most known for his artist pen name Beeple, was paid 69.3 million dollars during one NFT transaction of his work, ‘Winkelmann’s Everydays: The First 5000 Days.’ - a commission from Christie’s during an online auction that took place February 25 to March 11, 2021. It made people turn heads - afterall, 69.3 million dollars is nothing short of a success story.There’s also Trevor Jones from Edinburgh, Scotland, who has been merely a simple worker being paid minimum wage at age 30 yet has risen to fame and became a miracle success story essentially overnight, topping the highest bids on SuperRare and MakersPlace among other NFT Marketplaces. And they’re not the only ones - because the NFT Marker has no doubt changed lives and has given even newbie artists a way to monetize their work by thousands of dollars as long as someone is interested; and for some, there are. (Che Wei Peh, 2021.)



It’s impossible, after all, to deny how, despite how uncertain it is, when the cards are played right, it can be a very lucrative job. According to the rankings on OpenSea, a statistics-based website for NFTs, the average NFT price on MakersPlace is 0.87 eth, which is priced at $2,400 and for SuperRare, it’s 2.15 eth, which is $5,200. This data is heavily skewed by the outliers, though, but it’s still very profitable because the true average would still be around $300 give or take for small-scale transactions by relatively unknown artists. (Parker, 2021).

SAME, FAIR PLAYING FIELD

And, not only is it profitable, it also puts artists on the same fair playing field with a larger reach and audience compared to more traditional art collecting spaces like museums and exhibits. With NFT marketplaces, even young artists can monetize their art and have people, whether greatly well off (millionaires) or just having a few hundred dollars to spare (middle class) be potential clients and buyers. There is also no “algorithm” that favors the famous. In fact, Beeple, the artist who was able to earn 69.3 million dollars, was a millennial who just slowly began making a name for himself before the NFT Craze. And while it may just all come down to luck, it’s a very worthwhile toss of a coin to capitalize on this market to earn a few hundreds, or thousands, of extra dollars.

As Griffin Cock Foster, co-founder of Nifty Gateway, states, on being an artist, “It requires thinking outside the box, doing something that’s different than what anyone else has seen before in a way that gets you attention.” and those that are able to be just that in a very decentralized market such as the NFT scene may very well be able to reap the good effects of it compared to, for example, a much more unpredictable place like the art collector market.


COPYRIGHT AND PROTECTION

In the most simple terms, NFTs can help with protection and copyright of one work because after it has been minted (sold), it is attached to a blockchain, which is the NFT - a system of recording information to make it very difficult to hack and change - that records time-stamped metadata and all information on transactions - the people involved, how much was spent, the website it happened on, etc. (Jordan, 2021.)

This is greatly different from other ways of art protection like watermarking because as these watermarked artworks are posted on social media, someone can easily remove it by painting over the picture and/or cropping it out and no one would know until the original artist has been informed. Copyright in social media is also rarely taken seriously. For NFTs, however, the information is imprinted in its metadata and since it is bought through cryptocurrency with actual money, there is a bigger importance on its authenticity. There also is no work similar to it in the whole web, as NFTs are generally marketed to be unique and without any copies.

>


HOWEVER,

However, it isn’t full proof, because NFTs also provide a motive for art thieves to continue what they’re doing since, if done right, they’ll be earning money. With more incentive to steal, the rise of this is inescapable. Opportunism, sometimes, led to loss of morality and this act is nothing but blatant disrespect for all artists. And one could argue that the protection through the blockchain is protection enough, and that cases otherwise are the minority, but while the perks are good, it is unfortunately balanced by the added motivation and incentive for thieves to steal. And in the end, what is supposed to protect artists, does not always actually protect them. (Dish, 2021.)


PRIDE OF OWNERSHIP; UNIQUENESS

NFTs are “one of a kind” assets that do not exist anywhere else on the internet other than in the hands of those that buy it; therefore, it cannot be claimed by other people. This creates a sense of exclusivity that prompts people to purchase it for the pride of simply owning it, especially if it is attached to big, famous names like certain artists and musicians whose fans are willing to spend money on. An artist who fits this description and who has sold a few NFTs totalling to millions of dollars is Steve Aoki.

Then, the same with art collectors, NFTs can also be bought and resold if the buyer wishes to. This is because it is a property and asset that slowly rises in value throughout the years - akin to jewelry and paintings - which means that once it has been well-loved, it can be resold at a higher initial price than what was spent to buy it initially. This makes people who are interested in long-term investing have incentive to purchase it



DISADVANTAGES

The Anti-NFT Argument


There are really only two major reason for why NFTs are heavily criticized:

  • In terms of the environment, it is very detrimental because of how much energy (in teraWatts) it consumes, contributing to global warming
  • In terms of security, it encourages art theft and stealing

IT'S ENERGY CONSUMPTION; DETRIMENTAL TO ENVIRONMENT

According to Pforzheimer, “NFTs are the big, hot new fad” that allows for huge revenue and advancement in the digital art world “but comes with a massive cost to the environment.” With how cryptocurrency and the blockchain technology is massively reliant on its energy consumption to support its highly secure services, since its creation, NFTs, specifically Ethereum, is now said to be responsible for over 96,200,000 tons of CO2 - the combined annual carbon emissions for 84 countries in the world. This is according to Joanie Lemercier, a French artist whose work is deeply tied to climate activism. And yet it's only getting worse for the market has grown bigger than ever, and with the current rate of 1.25 million ETH transactions per day, it’s not far- fetched to say that what we are essentially doing is destroying our planet and enabling global warming.

However, before we dive into how this is linked to the NFT market, it is first important to understand what global warming really means and how carbon dioxide emissions contribute to making it worse.

WHAT IS GLOBAL WARMING?

Global Warming is essentially the heating of our world due to the production of carbon dioxide exceeding more than what natural processes can remove, making the Earth’s energy limit unbalanced and causing the atmosphere to trap additional heat in; consequently, the average temperature of our atmosphere is increased. (Lindsey, 2021.)

WHY IS IT IMPORTANT?

Carbon dioxide is directly connected to global warming and climate change because of its nature. It is a greenhouse gas - a gas that absorbs heat and releases it gradually over time, and according to Linsay, is “like bricks in a fireplace after a fire goes out”. And just like other gasses, we need to keep a balanced level in our atmosphere. However, according to the American Meteorological Society’s State of the Climate Report, the last 60 years have seen a growth rate in the amount of CO2 in the air around 100 times faster than before.

This isn’t just a matter of the atmosphere feeling more stuffy, but that it increases the chance of extreme weather events like droughts and heatwaves, and affects the climate of the world (the tropical seasons become even hotter and the winter areas start to melt.)

And the effects of this increase are being seen already. According to the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration (NOAA), the average temperature globally has increased 0.8 degrees Celsius in the past 100 years, and no doubt will it continue to rise. Then, we can see that in both poles, ice is melting and glaciers have drastically decreased in size and lakes are warming very rapidly. If this continues, animals living in these places will lose their habitats, leading to their eventual extinction. Human life, too, will be impacted because of the shift in climate. This is according to Josef Werne, professor of environmental science.


HOW MUCH DOES THE NFT MARKET CONTRIBUTE TO GLOBAL WARMING?

To put it into perspective, according to a survey done just recently, in October, by Raynold, 1 ETH transaction consumes more than the energy used in 100,000 VISA transactions. With every ETH transaction as well, it results in the emission of 33.4-48kg of CO2 according to Digiconomist Memo Akten. As mentioned earlier, too, Ethereum, since its creation, has resulted in over 92,600,000 tons of CO2 released into the atmosphere. And this is to emphasize that this is only for one ETH transaction, which we can scale to the current number of transactions per day which is 1.25 million on average and realize that, indeed, we’re enabling the release of over 40 million kg of Carbon Dioxide into our atmosphere, on top of other sources of it. And again, this is for what? For digital art?


The figure above shows the table for comparison of energy consumption (in kWh) of many transaction-based currencies, including Ethereum.



NOW, WHERE DO NFTs COME IN ALL THIS?

Where do NFTs come in all this? Simple. It’s massive carbon footprint along with how often it is being used equates to more release of carbon dioxide and consequently, the worsening of global warming.

In fact, the main reason for why Ethereum transactions consume so much energy is because of Ethereum mining. According to foundation, “mining is the process of creating a block of transactions to be added to the Ethereum blockchain” and this is done through a super-charged computer system software that spends days hashing at the codes to process transactions and blocks - and making sure they get to the etherium first. On average, to mine one ethereum, it would take 7.5 days for a very high quality software and that is highly dependent on the amount of etherium left mineable. It is no different from bitcoin mining, and that is why both of them share the same disadvantages of having exorbitant energy consumption

The NFT market blowing up equates to such a big percentage of why so many ETH transactions are taking place everyday, especially because despite all of this, people still choose to remain uneducated and instead, focus on how it’s a one way ticket to earning money. If the NFT market was to have never existed, the atmosphere would be 92,600,000 tons of CO2 lighter, and global warming might be less of a threat than it is right now. However, given how it has already happened, people are rallying to put an end to this market because continuing to operate it will no doubt have very bad effects on our environment top of what it has already caused.

And, for many people, the perks of giving artists a platform to monetize their work on a bigger scale does not outweigh the massive negative effects it has on our economy and climate.

PREVALANCE OF ART THEFT

On the other side of the spectrum for its advantage on maximum security, the market is also a breeding ground for art theft. This is because, as mentioned, people now have more incentive to steal art that has not been made into an NFT before (or minted) and sell them as one, with the profit going to the thief and not to the original artist. No one would know unless if it was to be called out, because blockchains can only detect duplicates within those already minted, not those just posted on social media.

In fact, there was a situation earlier in the year, May 2021, where Qing Han (most known for her artist pen name, Qinni), an artist who had unfortunately died in 2018, had her art stolen and sold as NFTs to the tune of $5,000 and upwards (Kwan, 2021.) It was nothing short of disrespect to the late artist who had been very passionate about her art.
